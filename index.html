People who feel socially excluded are especially prone to impostor syndrome...

  • First introduced in the 1970's as a result of studying successful women in high-powered careers who suffered from intense feelings of self doubt despite external successes
  • Most prominently affects marginalized groups of society such as minorities, LGBT, and members of lower socioeconomic classes

...but it can affect anyone.

  • When we first prepared to give this session at NYC Camp up in New York, we got feedback that surprised us from our male colleagues. Instead of saying, “That doesn’t sound like a real thing,” as I had personally expected, most said, “I’ve felt like that so often. I’d want to attend that.”
  • Even if you’ve studied web development formally, the technology is always changing, and it’s easy to get behind and feel like you’re now in a position you lucked into and don’t deserve. For those of us who are self-taught, this can be even more insidious.
  • I, for example, didn’t become a developer because I studied it. I became one because when I was 12, I taught myself HTML instead of playing sports. Almost 10 years later, I got a college internship coding email newsletters and quickly realized I loved development more than I loved the International Relations field my degree was in. Whoops.
  • A lot of men follow this same path. Being self-taught and stumbling into your field, where code is usually under the scrutiny of your peers, can give you the feeling that you don’t really deserve to be doing what you’re doing, even when you excel at it -- textbook impostor syndrome. In short: This might be more common in women and other minority groups, but it can affect anybody.

So, why is impostor syndrome a problem?

  • Self selecting out of pursuing advanced job opportunities means lower pay and less career advancement.
  • It can lead to higher levels of stress, long hours puts you at a much more severe risk of burnout.
  • In the big picture, it can lead to less representation among the ranks of decision-makers for women and minorities likely to be affected by impostor syndrome.

The community loses diversity.

  • Impostor syndrome is a trap, and the first step to overcoming it is to realize that even though you will fall into it, you can get out. Impostor syndrome is real, and you are not alone.

Give yourself permission to make mistakes.

  • When you’re in the self-doubt trap, the first and most important thing you should do is to give yourself permission to make mistakes -- because you’re going to make them whether you want to or not.
  • Alicia Liu, an engineer who has blogged and spoken about impostor syndrome, wrote that "programming is basically failing over and over again until it works, and that makes it hard to build confidence."
  • Our field is built on making mistakes and using StackOverflow to fix them. Once you let yourself off the hook for not being perfect at everything, the easier it gets to grow your skills.
  • You might be afraid to make mistakes because you think your coworkers, your bosses, or your teams have you under intense scrutiny because you’re not good enough, you’re not fast enough -- because you’re an impostor.
  • You might be working extra hard not to attract attention, and keeping your head down when you hear a term you don’t know.
  • You might be dodging opportunitites to try something you’re not certain you’ll be great at.

If you aren't making mistakes, it means you aren't out there doing anything.

Neil Gaiman

  • "What's the worst that could happen" is a cheeky slogan that one of our colleagues put as a tag line on one of our development tools.
  • Take down a production server? Accidentally publish something that wasn't supposed to go live yet? Delete important file?
  • One bug, one misstep, one failed project, one bad job choice is not going to ruin the rest of your career.
  • What matters is how you respond when things go awry.
  • Take responsibility, escalate early, dig in and fix things.
  • These can be hard lessons, but they will become war stories.
  • When you feel yourself down talking, or starting to panic, it can help to mentally go "Stop. This is the impostor syndrome talking." Try to retrain your mind this way.
  • Visualize yourself as the confident techy you see in other people.
  • It really helps to have someone to talk to about this, or at least start a journal. Get your feelings out and think about what brought them on.

Get active in the community.

  • By getting out into the community and talking to people of varying skill sets and ranks, you can get a better sense of where you fit in and more likely than not, you’ll realize you know more than you think you do.

Find (or build)
your support network.

  • Being able to talk openly about your fears and anxieties in a safe space where you feel comfortable sharing is key to helping you get over impostor syndrome. Realizing that not everybody has it all figured out-- not even people you perceive as the “experts”, who are so much better than you-- helps you recognize that you don’t have to suffer in a vacuum. It may be helpful for you to start attending local meetups to identify supportive networks of individuals.
  • Drupal Users Group at the University of Maryland. Discussed, presented and conduct demos and trainings to help disseminate knowledge within our group and also to provide support for one another. Not only did I learn so much from my group members and was continually inspired to learn more, as a I leader I had to recognize that I was being perceived as an authority on Drupal, a position which I eventually had to own.

This gives you a better understanding of your own skills!

  • Kat: If you're self-taught, it's easy to think that everyone else must be better than you. Once you start meeting others in the community, you might go into it thinking that everyone knows more than you do -- but you're going to realize that you know a lot more than you thought.

Be a mentor.

  • Source: Pacific Standard
  • Katherine Anderson, who works in finance in New York, learned about a new mentoring program for women who work in venture capital and private equity. As a mid-level manager who’d struggled to find good mentorship, she was thrilled. “I said how much I'd like to participate,” Anderson told me, “preferably on the mentee side of the program since I don't think there is much I can offer to someone else.”
  • The program organizer laughed and told her that she’d just had a high-level managing director at a private equity firm say the exact same thing. None of the women who’d signed up to attend—regardless of their title—thought they were experienced enough to be mentors. “It's sort of surprising to me that even when you get to the top, you still don't realize you've made it there.”

What do mentors do?

  • Offer their time.
  • Review code/development with individuals one-on-one.
  • Listen to presentations and give feedback.
  • Give career advice and consultation.

Speak.

  • Once you find the skills or topics that come naturally for you and start sharing your knowledge informally, you might realize that you want to do it on a larger scale. With both local and national events, the open source community -- and the Drupal community in particular -- makes it relatively easy to get started with speaking.

Remember: You (yes, you) can do this.

  • The first thing to realize is that you can, and should, speak even if it makes you nervous.
  • I remember meeting webchick for the first time at the Denver DrupalCon, my first DrupalCon. We talked at a bar and she told me that I should speak at events. And I was really surprised, because hell, it was my first time even going to DrupalCon, why would I speak at one? I briefly thought that I had accidentally misled her about my experience.
  • But the next time a speaking opportunity came up, I took it, and I loved it. This session is the 9th one I’ve spoken at, on topics ranging from front-end to migration to site building to client management.

Find your own spin on the topic.

Remind yourself that feedback will
make your first presentation stronger.

  • If you want to speak but you think, like I did, that you’re going to crash and burn if you try, know that there are a lot of feedback loops you can use before you get up on stage.
  • You can get a topic and talk to that support network you built to see if it has legs.
  • You can put a session proposal together and see if it’s what the event is looking for -- and if it gets accepted, congrats, it was!
  • You can do a dry-run of the presentation for people you trust.
  • In short, if you’re attracted to speaking, do it; when you do things that scare you, you find out what you love.

Own your career.

Decide what you want;
then do your research.

If you want to move up at your current job...

Tell your boss what skills you want to grow.

Learn the responsibilities (& job description)
of the role you want.

Keep a record of your accomplishments
(& take credit for them).

Don’t say:

  • "I just Googled it."
  • "It's no big deal."
  • "She did most of the work."

Say instead:

  • Thank you!

If you want a new job...

Tap your network.

Don't hesitate to apply
for what you want.

Hewlett Packard report:

  • Women wouldn't apply for a job unless they felt they meet 100% of the qualifications.
  • Men applied if they felt they meet as little as 60% of them.

Don't spend your time explaining
what you don't know in an interview.

(Other people aren't.)

Know why you're a great hire.
Then tell that story at your interview.
